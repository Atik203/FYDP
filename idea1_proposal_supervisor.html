FYDP · Idea 1 — Research Proposal

Trust-Calibrated Multi-Agent Scientific Deliberation
for Mitigating Sycophantic Consensus in LLM Reasoning

Final Year Design Project — Research Proposal

Domain: Agentic AI · Multi-Agent Systems · Trustworthy NLP  · 

1. Background and Motivation

Getting several large language models (LLMs) to debate a question before agreeing on an answer — Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) — is a popular way to improve factual accuracy (Du et al., 2023). But these debates have a real weakness: an agent often drops a correct answer just to agree with a confident-sounding majority, and the group settles on a confidently wrong consensus (Yao et al., 2025). Because today's models are trained on overlapping data, they tend to make the same mistakes, so a plain majority vote can quietly bury a correct minority — the minority is actually right in about one in four disagreements (He et al., 2026). In scientific or clinical question answering, a confidently wrong group answer is more harmful than an honest "I'm not sure."

2. Problem Statement

In current debate systems, an agent's influence on the final answer depends on how many other agents agree with it — not on whether what it says is actually true. There is no mechanism that adjusts each agent's influence during the debate based on whether its claims can be checked against real scientific evidence. As a result, a hallucinating majority can override a correct, well-supported minority. This is the problem we want to solve.

3. Research Gap

Recent work has measured this failure (Yao et al., 2025; He et al., 2026) and built efficiency-focused debate controllers (iMAD, Fan et al., 2025). What is still missing is a mechanism that mitigates it: an evidence-grounded trust rule that re-weights agents during the debate, plus a reproducible way to measure whether it helps under pressure. That gap is what this project targets.

4. Our Idea (Core of the Project)

A group of open-weight LLMs from different families debate a question. As they argue, each agent's claims are broken into small factual statements and checked against scientific sources (Semantic Scholar, arXiv, PubMed). Each agent then carries a trust score that goes up when its claims are supported by evidence and down when they are contradicted, kept within bounds so no agent is silenced or allowed to dominate. Instead of a plain majority vote, the final answer is produced by trust-weighted aggregation — so a well-supported minority can survive a confident but unsupported majority. A lightweight check triggers the full debate only for uncertain questions, keeping cost low.

5. Research Question and Objectives

Research question: when a debate faces a planted, confident-but-wrong majority, does evidence-grounded trust weighting keep correct minority answers alive better than a plain majority vote and other common methods?

  1. Design a bounded, evidence-weighted trust-update rule driven by supported vs. contradicted claims.
  2. Build the pipeline: claim decomposition → evidence retrieval → scoring → trust-weighted aggregation.
  3. Create a small, reproducible evaluation setup — a controlled way to inject a false consensus plus three metrics (CCR, MPR, ECR).
  4. Compare against strong baselines and report honestly, including where it does not help.

6. High-Level Methodology

Models: three agents from different open-weight families (≈14–32B), run locally with vLLM; exact checkpoints fixed early against whatever is released and stable. No training — everything runs at inference time.

Evidence: claims are split into atomic statements, evidence is retrieved from academic sources, and a cross-encoder scores it; unverifiable claims are set aside, not trusted.

Datasets: BrokenMath, BrokenArXiv, HLE for the adversarial sycophancy tests; GPQA, MMLU-Pro for ordinary-accuracy comparison.

Evaluation: a controlled false-consensus injection, validated with an inter-annotator agreement check, then results over 3 seeds with 95% confidence intervals and a small human study to sanity-check the metrics.

Metrics: CCR — how often a correct agent collapses to the wrong answer; MPR — how often a correct minority survives to the output; ECR — how well an agent's trust matches whether it is actually right.

Baselines: single agent (±retrieval), standard MAD (±retrieval), self-consistency, Mixture-of-Agents, and a confidence-triggered debate in the style of iMAD.

7. Expected Contributions

  • An evidence-grounded trust-weighting mechanism that reduces sycophantic consensus collapse in debate.
  • An open, reusable evaluation setup (injection protocol + CCR/MPR/ECR + baselines).
  • A clear picture of when evidence-grounded trust helps and when it does not, with failure analysis.

8. Expected Outcomes

  • A working prototype with released code, prompts, and protocol.
  • A measurable reduction in correct-minority collapse over strong baselines, while staying competitive on standard accuracy.
  • A solid thesis and a paper suitable for a good conference or journal in the area.

9. Scope and Feasibility

It is inference-only, runs on a single A100-class GPU, and uses open-weight models with free academic retrieval APIs. It does not try to eliminate hallucination or solve alignment in general — it focuses on the specific, measurable problem of reducing sycophantic consensus in scientific QA. The main risks are retrieval quality on niche questions and cases where all agents share the same misconception, and we plan to study both explicitly rather than assume them away.

10. Key References (what they did)

  • Du et al. (2023) — introduced multi-agent debate; showed debate improves factual and reasoning accuracy. (Our base method.)
  • Yao et al. (2025) — showed and measured that agents become sycophantic in debate and collapse to a wrong majority. (Defines the problem we mitigate.)
  • He et al. (2026) — showed the correct minority is suppressed by a correlated majority in ~1 of 4 disagreements. (Motivates protecting the minority.)
  • Fan et al. (2025), iMAD — learns when to trigger debate for efficiency. (A baseline; it does not decide who to trust.)
  • Wang et al. (2024), Mixture-of-Agents — combines multiple models with equal-weight aggregation. (A baseline; no evidence-based trust.)
