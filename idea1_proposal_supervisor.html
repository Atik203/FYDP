Trust-Calibrated Multi-Agent Scientific Deliberation
for Mitigating Sycophantic Consensus in LLM Reasoning

Final Year Design Project — Research Proposal

Domain: Agentic AI · Multi-Agent Systems · Trustworthy NLP  · 

1. Background and Motivation

Getting several large language models (LLMs) to debate a question before settling on a joint answer — usually called Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) — has become a common way to push up factual and reasoning accuracy (Du et al., 2023). The idea is appealing: different models catch each other's mistakes. In practice, though, these debates have a worrying weakness. Recent studies show that an agent will often drop a correct answer just to agree with a confident-sounding majority, and the whole debate then settles on a wrong but confident consensus (Yao et al., 2025). Because today's models are trained on largely overlapping data, they tend to make the same mistakes, so a simple majority vote can quietly bury a correct minority — He et al. (2026) find the minority is actually right in about one in four disagreements. In areas like scientific or clinical question answering, a confidently wrong group answer is more harmful than an honest "I'm not sure."

2. Problem Statement

In current debate systems, how much an agent influences the final answer comes down to how many other agents happen to agree with it — not whether what it is saying is actually true. There is no mechanism that adjusts each agent's influence, within the same debate, based on whether its specific claims can be checked against real scientific evidence. As a result, a hallucinating majority can override a correct, well-supported minority. This project sets out to fix that.

3. Research Gap

The problem itself is by now fairly well documented and even measured (Yao et al., 2025; He et al., 2026), and there are debate controllers that focus on efficiency, such as iMAD (Fan et al., 2025) and HCP-MAD (2026). What is still missing is a properly specified, evidence-grounded trust mechanism that re-weights agents during the debate based on how verifiable their claims are — together with a reproducible way to actually measure whether such a mechanism helps when a debate is put under sycophantic pressure. This is the gap I want to address.

4. Research Question and Objectives

Research question: when a debate is exposed to a planted, confident-but-wrong majority, does evidence-grounded trust weighting hold on to correct minority answers better than plain majority vote, self-consistency, Mixture-of-Agents, and confidence-weighted debate?

To answer this, the objectives are:

  1. Design a bounded, evidence-weighted trust-update rule that raises or lowers an agent's influence based on how many of its claims are supported or contradicted by retrieved evidence.
  2. Build the surrounding pipeline: break each answer into small checkable claims, retrieve evidence for them, score that evidence, and combine the agents' answers using their trust rather than a raw vote.
  3. Put together a small, reproducible evaluation setup — a controlled way of injecting a false consensus, plus three metrics (CCR, MPR, ECR) — so the effect can be measured fairly.
  4. Compare the method against strong baselines and the closest recent work, and report the results carefully, including the cases where it does not help.

5. Proposed Solution

A group of open-weight LLMs from different families debate a question. As they argue, each agent's claims are split into small factual statements and checked against scientific sources (for example Semantic Scholar, arXiv, and PubMed) using a cross-encoder to score how relevant the evidence is. Each agent carries a trust score that goes up when its claims are backed by evidence and down when they are contradicted, kept within sensible bounds so that no single agent is silenced and none is allowed to completely dominate. Instead of taking a plain majority vote at the end, the system weights the agents by their trust, which lets a well-supported minority answer survive against a confident but unsupported majority. A lightweight confidence check decides when the full debate is even needed, so easy questions are answered directly and compute is not wasted. Because the trust rule, the retrieval, and the aggregation are separate pieces, each one can be switched off in ablation studies to see how much it actually contributes.

6. High-Level Methodology

Models. Three agents drawn from different open-weight families (roughly 14–32B parameters), run locally with vLLM; the exact checkpoints will be fixed early in the project against whatever is released and stable at that point. A stronger API model is used only as an upper-bound reference, not as one of the debating agents. No model training is involved — the whole system runs at inference time.

Evidence. Each claim is decomposed into atomic statements, evidence is retrieved from academic sources, and a cross-encoder scores it. Claims that cannot be verified are set aside rather than trusted, so the system leans towards caution.

Datasets. BrokenMath, BrokenArXiv, and HLE are used for the adversarial sycophancy tests, while GPQA and MMLU-Pro give a stable point of comparison for ordinary accuracy.

Evaluation. The core of the evaluation is a controlled way of injecting a wrong "expert consensus" into the debate, validated with an inter-annotator agreement check before the main runs. Results are reported over three random seeds with 95% confidence intervals and effect sizes, plus a small human study (around 60 samples) to sanity-check the automatic metrics.

Metrics. CCR measures how often a correct agent collapses to the injected wrong answer; MPR measures how often a correct minority survives to the final output; and ECR measures how well an agent's trust lines up with whether it is actually right.

Baselines. Single agent (with and without retrieval), standard MAD (with and without retrieval), self-consistency, Mixture-of-Agents, and a re-implemented confidence-triggered debate in the style of iMAD. The recent diagnosis work (Peacemaker/Troublemaker, Minority Sentinel) is also compared against directly.

7. Expected Contributions

  • An evidence-grounded, bounded trust-weighting mechanism that reduces sycophantic consensus collapse during multi-agent debate.
  • An open, reproducible evaluation setup — the injection protocol along with the CCR, MPR, and ECR metrics and the baseline suite — that others can reuse.
  • A clear empirical picture of when evidence-grounded trust actually helps and when it does not, backed by failure analysis.

8. Expected Outcomes

  • A working prototype together with released code, prompts, and the evaluation protocol.
  • A measurable drop in correct-minority collapse compared with strong baselines on the adversarial benchmarks, while staying competitive on the standard ones.
  • A thesis and a paper aimed at a workshop or EMNLP-Findings-level venue, with an IEEE/Springer conference or a Q2 journal as realistic alternatives.

9. Scope and Feasibility

The project is deliberately kept feasible for an FYDP: it is inference only, runs on a single A100-class GPU, and relies on open-weight models and free academic retrieval APIs. It does not try to eliminate hallucination or solve alignment in general — it focuses on the specific, measurable problem of reducing sycophantic consensus in scientific question answering. I expect the main risks to be retrieval quality on niche questions and cases where all agents happen to share the same misconception, and I plan to study both explicitly rather than assume them away.
